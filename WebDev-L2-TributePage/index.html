Biography

A Life Spent Looking Closer

Her laboratory notebooks are still radioactive today and are kept in lead-lined boxes at the Bibliothèque nationale de France.

She and Pierre refused to patent the radium-isolation process, so any laboratory in the world could use it freely.

Albert Einstein reportedly said she was probably the only person whom fame could never corrupt.

Maria Skłodowska was born in Warsaw in 1867, the youngest of five children of two schoolteachers. Russian authorities controlled Congress Poland at the time and had stripped laboratory work from the curriculum, so her father smuggled instruments home to teach his children science in secret. By the time Maria was eleven she had lost both her mother and her eldest sister, and she had also discovered that she was, by a wide margin, the sharpest student in any room she entered. Barred from university simply for being a woman, she studied physics through an underground network of classes Poles called the "Flying University," and tutored for years to save enough money to follow her sister to Paris.

Before any of that, though, came two quieter heartbreaks. Her sister Zofia had died of typhus caught from a lodger the family took in for income, and a few years later, while working as a governess to fund her future studies, Maria fell in love with the family's son, a young mathematician named Kazimierz Żorawski. His parents refused to let him marry a penniless relative, and he was unable to stand up to them. Decades later, as an old man and a professor himself, he was known to sit quietly in front of a statue of Maria that had been raised outside the Warsaw institute she founded — a small, sad postscript to a romance that never got to happen.

She arrived at the Sorbonne in 1891, half-frozen in a garret apartment and living on bread and tea, and finished at the top of her class anyway. A mutual interest in laboratory space introduced her to Pierre Curie, a physicist who, in her own telling, was the first person who seemed to take her work as seriously as she did. They married in 1895 in plain clothes with no religious ceremony, and the dark dress Marie wore that day doubled as her lab coat for years afterward. When Henri Becquerel announced that uranium gave off mysterious rays on its own, with no outside light source, Marie chose it as the subject of her doctoral thesis — a decision that would consume the rest of her life.

Working out of a leaking, unheated shed because no one would give them a proper laboratory, Marie and Pierre processed tonnes of a mineral called pitchblende, ounce by ounce, to chase a signal her instruments told her had to be there. In 1898 they announced two new elements: polonium, named for her occupied homeland, and radium, from the Latin for "ray." Along the way she coined the word "radioactivity" itself. Neither of them yet understood that the substances glowing softly on their shelves at night were poisoning them. In 1903 the Curies shared the Nobel Prize in Physics with Becquerel, making Marie the first woman ever to receive one — though, as a woman, she was not permitted to speak at the Royal Institution lecture announcing their own work; Pierre delivered it alone.

Pierre was killed by a horse-drawn cart in 1906, and Marie, devastated, took over his chair at the Sorbonne — becoming its first female professor. Five years later her achievements were nearly overshadowed by scandal: it became public that she'd had a relationship with a married former student of Pierre's, and the right-wing French press, already inclined to paint her as a foreign outsider, tried to brand her a homewrecker just weeks before the Nobel committee was set to honor her again. Curie refused to be shamed out of attending. "The prize has been given to me for the discovery of polonium and radium," she wrote back to the committee chair, "there is no relation between my scientific work and my private life." She collected her second Nobel Prize, in Chemistry, in 1911 — for isolating pure radium — making her the only person in history honored in two different sciences.

During the First World War she taught herself auto mechanics and radiology so she could drive mobile X-ray units, nicknamed "Little Curies," to the front lines herself, with her teenage daughter Irène often working beside her. Her units are estimated to have helped treat over a million wounded soldiers. She went on to found the Radium Institute in Paris in 1920 and a sister institute in Warsaw in 1932, mentoring a generation of scientists — many of them women — before dying in 1934 of aplastic anaemia, almost certainly a consequence of the very radiation exposure she had spent her life studying. Her legacy didn't end there: in 1935, her daughter Irène Joliot-Curie won her own Nobel Prize in Chemistry, making the Curies the most decorated family in the history of the award.

Milestones

Key Discoveries & Turning Points

1891

Arrives in Paris

Enrolls at the Sorbonne to study physics and mathematics, after years of saving money as a governess in Poland.

1895

Marries Pierre Curie

The two begin a scientific partnership that will define both of their careers and change the course of physics.

1898

Discovers polonium and radium

After processing tonnes of pitchblende ore by hand, the Curies announce two new elements and coin the term "radioactivity."

1903

First Nobel Prize

Shares the Nobel Prize in Physics with Pierre and Henri Becquerel, becoming the first woman to win a Nobel Prize.

1906

First woman professor at the Sorbonne

After Pierre's sudden death in a street accident, Marie takes over his professorship at the University of Paris.

1911

Weathers a press scandal

Her relationship with physicist Paul Langevin becomes public; the French press attacks her as a foreign outsider days before her second Nobel.

1911

Second Nobel Prize

Wins the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for isolating pure radium — still the only person honored in two different sciences.

1914–1918

Mobile X-ray units at the front

Develops and personally drives "Little Curies" — mobile radiography vehicles — to treat wounded soldiers near the front lines.

1920

Founds the Radium Institute, Paris

Establishes a dedicated radioactivity research institute, later renamed the Institut Curie, training a new generation of scientists.

1921

Tours the United States

Raises funds for research and is given one gram of radium by President Warren Harding on behalf of American women.

1932

Founds the Radium Institute, Warsaw

Opens a sister institute in her native city, with her own sister Bronisława installed as its first director.

1934

Death from radiation exposure

Dies of aplastic anaemia at a sanatorium in the French Alps, almost certainly a consequence of decades of unprotected research.

Legacy

What She Left Behind

84Po

Polonium

Named after her native Poland, then still partitioned among three empires with no state of its own.

88Ra

Radium

Its faint glow in the dark drawer of her desk hinted at a danger no one yet understood.

Unit

The curie (Ci)

A unit of radioactivity named for Marie and Pierre, still used in nuclear medicine today.

1995

Reinterred at the Panthéon

Became the first woman honored at the Paris Panthéon on her own scientific merit, sealed in lead for safety.

96Cm

Curium

A synthetic element discovered in 1944 was named curium in honor of both Marie and Pierre Curie.

Family

A dynasty of laureates

Daughter Irène Joliot-Curie won the 1935 Chemistry Nobel with her husband Frédéric — five Nobel Prizes across one family.

Worldwide

Honored on every continent

Curie has appeared on more than 600 postage stamps, several banknotes, a 2024 French euro coin, and a 2011 Google Doodle.

EU programme

Marie Skłodowska-Curie Actions

The European Union's fellowship for early-career researchers abroad carries her name, funding scientists who follow her path today.
